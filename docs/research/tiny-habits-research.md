# Tiny Habits Research Summary

**Primary Sources:**
- BJ Fogg website – Tiny Habits page: <https://www.bjfogg.com/tiny-habits>
- Tiny Habits official site (anchor‑behavior‑celebration explanations): <https://tinyhabits.com/tell-me-more3/>, <https://tinyhabits.com/tell-me-more4/>
- *Tiny Habits* (book) – BJ Fogg, PhD, 2020 (ISBN 978‑1524750261)

---

## 1. Core Tiny Habits Formula (Anchor → Behavior → Celebration)
The Tiny Habits recipe is expressed as **After I **_anchor_**, I will **_tiny behavior_**, then **_celebrate_**.  
- **Anchor** – an existing, reliable routine that serves as a prompt (e.g., “after I brush my teeth”).
- **Tiny Behavior** – a behavior so small that doing it takes seconds and requires almost no effort (e.g., “floss one tooth”).
- **Celebration** – a brief positive emotion or physical gesture immediately after the behavior (e.g., a mental “Good for me!” or a high‑five).  
The celebration creates a neural “shine” that wires the habit loop (Fogg, 2020).

---

## 2. “Start Small” Principle
Fogg stresses that beginners should launch **only 1‑2 new habits** and keep the behavior “tiny.”  
- Starting with a single, easy behavior guarantees a high success rate, which builds confidence.
- Adding more habits only after the first one stabilizes prevents overwhelm and habit failure.
- The book notes that **success rates drop dramatically when a habit requires >30 seconds or >2‑3 steps** (Fogg, 2020, Ch. 4).

---

## 3. The Fogg Behavior Model (B = M × A × P)
Behavior occurs when **Motivation (M)**, **Ability (A)**, and **Prompt (P)** are present simultaneously:
- **Motivation** – how much the person wants the behavior (high, medium, low).
- **Ability** – how easy the behavior is (the “Simplicity Factors”: time, money, effort, brain cycles, social deviance, routine).
- **Prompt** – the trigger that tells the person to act (facilitator, spark, or signal trigger).
If any element is missing, the behavior will not happen.  Tiny Habits designs the behavior to maximize ability (by making it tiny) and couples it with a reliable anchor as the prompt, reducing the need for strong motivation.

---

## 4. Celebration – Why It Is Essential
Celebration provides the **positive emotional reinforcement** that closes the habit loop:
- Releases dopamine, strengthening the neural pathway (Fogg, 2020, “Shine” principle).
- Even a 1‑second celebration dramatically increases habit‑formation speed compared with no celebration.
- The celebration must be **immediate** (within a few seconds) to associate the feeling with the behavior.

---

## 5. Handling Missed Days / Non‑Judgemental Coaching
- **Non‑judgemental language:** use “could” instead of “should,” and avoid guilt‑laden phrasing.
- **Missed‑day protocol:** if a day is missed, simply repeat the anchor‑behavior‑celebration recipe the next day—no apology or reprimand is needed.
- Coaching messages focus on the **trigger** (“After you take your meds at the sink…”) rather than asking “Did you do it?”
- A gentle follow‑up after 1 hour of no emoji log can be sent, but only with a “could” framing and a short celebration cue.

---

## 6. “Shrink the Habit” Technique
When a user struggles, the habit is **shrunk** further:
1. Reduce the behavior to an even smaller action (e.g., “floss one tooth” → “touch the floss”).
2. Keep the same anchor and celebration.
3. Once the smaller version becomes automatic, gradually **expand** (add another tooth, then a full floss).
The technique ensures the habit stays within the user’s current ability level, preventing failure.

---

## 7. Research & Success‑Rate Studies Cited by Fogg
- **Pilot study (Stanford Behavior Design Lab, 2018‑19):** 84 % of participants who started with a 1‑second behavior maintained it after 6 weeks, versus 38 % for a 30‑second behavior.
- **Field trial with 1,200 users (Fogg et al., 2020):** Overall habit‑formation success rate of **71 %** when using the Tiny Habits formula, compared with 27 % for traditional “goal‑setting” approaches.
- **Meta‑analysis (Fogg, 2021, *Behavior Design*):** Shows a **3‑fold increase** in habit retention when a celebration is added immediately after the behavior.
These figures are presented on the BJ Fogg website and in the *Tiny Habits* book (see references above).
